## ✉️ Task 4 — Professional Email Communication

**Folder:** `Task 4/` &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Files:** `Task 4.docx`, `report4.docx`, `Social-media-chehcklist.txt`

**What Was Done — Three Deliverables:**

**1. Professional Email Drafts** (`Task 4.docx`) — Two complete formal emails:
- *Deadline Extension Request* — to a professor, requesting more time for the Digital Literacy project submission, with proper subject line, context, and sign-off
- *Summer Internship Inquiry* — a cold email to an internship coordinator expressing interest in a role, attaching a resume, and requesting next steps

**2. Communication Analysis** (`report4.docx`) — A short write-up examining how a brief, tone-deaf message (*"Send this now"*) in a workplace context causes misunderstandings, project delays, and team conflict — and what a professional version looks like.

**3. Social Media Checklist** (`Social-media-chehcklist.txt`) — Quick-reference Do's and Don'ts for responsible social media behaviour:

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Think before you post | Share offensive or harmful content |
| Maintain respectful communication | Overshare personal or sensitive info |
| Verify facts before sharing | Engage in online arguments or trolling |
| Use professional language on public profiles | Spread misinformation or rumours |
| Protect your privacy settings | Post content that can harm your reputation |

**Key Learning:**
Professional email writing is not formality for its own sake. Each element — subject line, salutation, body, sign-off — serves a specific function. Informal habits from daily messaging apps don't transfer to formal contexts, and in a professional setting the consequences of getting this wrong are much harder to undo than a quick apology in a chat window.

## 🔐 Task 5 — Cybercrime Awareness

**Folder:** `Task 5/` &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Files:** `casestudy.pdf`, `prevention-checklist.md`, `project-report-reflection.md`

### Case Study — UPI / Online Payment Fraud (`casestudy.pdf`)

The case study follows *Aryan*, a college student selling his laptop on OLX. A fraudster posing as a buyer sends a **UPI Collect Request** — a debit instruction — framed as a link to "receive" payment. Aryan enters his PIN and ₹18,000 is instantly debited. The money moves through a mule account and is untraceable within minutes.

**Core insight:** Most UPI fraud requires no hacking. The victim authorises the transaction themselves, having been manipulated into confusing the *Send* and *Collect* flows. NPCI data shows only ~22% of reported cases result in any refund.

**Step-by-step breakdown:**

| Step | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| 1 — Lure | Fraudster contacts Aryan on OLX, agrees to pay via UPI |
| 2 — False Request | Sends a QR code / link claiming "scan to receive money" |
| 3 — Deception | Aryan enters his UPI PIN thinking he is confirming receipt |
| 4 — Theft | ₹18,000 debited instantly from Aryan's account |
| 5 — Untraceable | Money moved through mule account and withdrawn within minutes |

---

### Prevention Checklist (`prevention-checklist.md`)

A 12-point checklist covering four areas:

**General Safety:** Strong unique passwords · Enable 2FA · Don't click unsolicited links · Verify URLs · Keep apps updated · Use VPN on public Wi-Fi

**UPI & Financial Safety *(Critical)*:**
> ⚠️ **NEVER enter your UPI PIN to "receive" money.** Your PIN is only needed when you SEND — never to receive.
>
> ⚠️ **Always read Collect Requests carefully** — they debit your account, not credit it.

**Social Media & Identity:** Audit privacy settings · Don't overshare personal details publicly

**How to Report Cybercrime in India:**

| Channel | Details |
|---|---|
| 🌐 National Portal | [cybercrime.gov.in](https://cybercrime.gov.in) |
| 📞 Helpline | **1930** — call immediately for financial fraud |
| 🏠 Local Cyber Cell | Nearest police station |
| 📧 Email | cybercrime@nic.in |

> **Tip:** Report UPI fraud within the **first hour** — banks may be able to freeze the receiving account before the money is withdrawn.

---

### Personal Reflection (`project-report-reflection.md`)

> *"The most surprising finding was how little technical skill is required to commit UPI fraud. The most damaging attacks rely entirely on social engineering — exploiting the confusion between Send and Collect flows. The victim themselves approves the transaction. No system is broken into at all."*

**One habit change:** Never approve a UPI Collect Request unless 100% certain it was self-initiated — regardless of how urgent the caller sounds.
